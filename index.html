National Cyber League (NCL) and Cyber Fusion Training Information for Fall 2017:
Sponsored by AllCyber
​
Training for the NCL and Cyber Fusion competitions will be the same. There is no previous NCL or Cyber Fusion experience required to participate in the NCL or Cyber Fusion competitions.  However, if you try out for the Cyber Fusion team, you must have competed in the Fall 2017 NCL season. The recommendations to prepare for NCL Fall Season include:
  • Go to https://www.nationalcyberleague.org/fall-season and look at the schedule
  • Go to http://cyberfusion.virginia.gov/ and browser the site (this is site from last year's event)
  • Learn the lingo at https://www.nationalcyberleague.org/fall-season (Preseason, Regular Season, etc.)
  • Subscribe to NCL's YouTube Channel at https://www.youtube.com/user/NationalCyberLeague
  • Work through sample challenges at sample challenges from past NCL game challenges​
  • Familiarize yourself with the NCL Stadium at https://www.nationalcyberleague.org/stadium
  • Understand how Scoring works at https://www.nationalcyberleague.org/scoring
  • Once the Gym https://www.nationalcyberleague.org/ncl-gymnasiums opens, start working through the lab exercises
  • Go to https://www.nationalcyberleague.org/lab-exercises to see the exercises you will complete in the Preseason
Competencies being measured in the NCL Preseason game (this is what you need to be studying, and we will teach, to compete in the NCL Preseason):
  • Open Source Intelligence
  • Network Traffic Analysis
  • Log Analysis
  • Cryptography
Competencies being measured in the NCL Regular Season game (this is what you need to be studying, and we will teach, to compete in the Regular Season):
  • ​Open Source Intelligence
  • Network Traffic Analysis
  • Scanning & Reconnaissance
  • Password Cracking
  • Web Application Exploitation
  • Log Analysis
  • Wireless Access Exploitation
  • Cryptography
  • Enumeration & Exploitation

  • Open Source Intelligence

Enigma

Learning Outcome(s)

When you complete the lab, you should be able to understand cryptography

Requirements

This project uses an Enigma® machine simulator. It functions like the Enigma machines used during WWII. This example has been included to help you better understand how encryption worked in the early days. It’s a great learning tool for when you first start exploring the subject of cryptography. Enigma machines provided fairly good encryption strength for their day. Modern cryptographic systems are much more secure than Enigma machines. Pay attention to the colored paths as you type. The red path goes through the three rotors, bounces off the reflector, becomes green, and then goes back through the three rotors. The right rotor moves with each keystroke. If it completes one full cycle, it will advance the middle rotor and subsequently the left rotor.

  • Open a Web browser and go to http://enigmaco.de/enigma/enigma.swf
  • Use the left and right arrows to move each of the top three rotors so that each has the letter “A” selected in blue.
  • Click in the Input text box in the bottom of your screen.
  • Slowly type your first name and last name without a space. (In this case it was RandyBoyle. If you make a typing error you can start over by pressing the backspace key.)
  • Take a screen capture.
    • Note: The text in the Input text box is what you typed. The text in the Output text box is what you would send. You are now going to reset the dials to their original position (in this case AAA) and type the encrypted text (cipher text) you produced in the Output text box. You can copy the cipher text from the screenshot you just took. Subsequently, you should see your name reproduced in the bottom box. This is the equivalent of decrypting the message.
  • Click in the Input text box and backspace your name. (The rotors should be set back to their AAA position.)
  • Refer back to the screenshot you just took and copy down the output (cipher text). (In this case, the cipher text for “RANDYBOYLE” was “VDOLZYMEAC.”)
  • Type the cipher text into the Input text box. (Type slowly so you won't make a mistake and have to start over!)
  • Take a screenshot with your name showing in the Output text box.10. Backspace the text in the Input text box.
  • Slowly press the A key ten times and notice how a different encrypted letter is chosen as output through the rotating dials even though you are hitting the same key each time.
  • Take a screen capture.
